“My child,” said the Doctor, rising, and laying his hand upon her shoulder, “I _have_ saved him. What weakness is this, my dear! Let me go to the door.”  He took the lamp in his hand, crossed the two intervening outer rooms, and opened it. A rude clattering of feet over the floor, and four rough men in red caps, armed with sabres and pistols, entered the room.  “The Citizen Evrémonde, called Darnay,” said the first.  “Who seeks him?” answered Darnay.  “I seek him. We seek him. I know you, Evrémonde; I saw you before the Tribunal to-day. You are again the prisoner of the Republic.”  The four surrounded him, where he stood with his wife and child clinging to him.  “Tell me how and why am I again a prisoner?”  “It is enough that you return straight to the Conciergerie, and will know to-morrow. You are summoned for to-morrow.”  Doctor Manette, whom this visitation had so turned into stone, that he stood with the lamp in his hand, as if he were a statue made to hold it, moved after these words were spoken, put the lamp down, and confronting the speaker, and taking him, not ungently, by the loose front of his red woollen shirt, said:  “You know him, you have said. Do you know me?”  “Yes, I know you, Citizen Doctor.”  “We all know you, Citizen Doctor,” said the other three.  He looked abstractedly from one to another, and said, in a lower voice, after a pause:  “Will you answer his question to me then?
