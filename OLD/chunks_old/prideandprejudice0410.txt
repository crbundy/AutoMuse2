Jane, I take       your place now, and you must go lower, because I am a married       woman.”        It was not to be supposed that time would give Lydia that       embarrassment from which she had been so wholly free at first.       Her ease and good spirits increased. She longed to see Mrs.       Phillips, the Lucases, and all their other neighbours, and to       hear herself called “Mrs. Wickham” by each of them; and in the       mean time, she went after dinner to show her ring, and boast of       being married, to Mrs. Hill and the two housemaids.        “Well, mamma,” said she, when they were all returned to the       breakfast room, “and what do you think of my husband? Is not he a       charming man? I am sure my sisters must all envy me. I only hope       they may have half my good luck. They must all go to Brighton.       That is the place to get husbands. What a pity it is, mamma, we       did not all go.”        “Very true; and if I had my will, we should. But my dear Lydia, I       don’t at all like your going such a way off. Must it be so?”        “Oh, lord! yes;—there is nothing in that. I shall like it of all       things. You and papa, and my sisters, must come down and see us.
